Car

Mechanical Design

The body of the car consists of a piece of acrylic with drilled holes to screw in the wheels and motors. Attached are two bigger front wheels, each controlled by a DC motor, and one smaller, unpowered back wheel for balance. The Arduino R3 and battery are attached to the top and bottom, respectively, of the car body with a velcro strap (with electrical tape on the bottom of the Arduino to prevent accidental shorting between the metal pins and the metal screws in the car).

Fig. Right: drone from directly above

Electrical Design

The car wheels are driven by one DC motor per wheel. These motors plug into an Adafruit Motor Shield with one wire in Vdd and the other in ground. The Adafruit Motor Shield attaches to each pin of the Arduino R3 pin connectors. 

The radio communicates with the Arduino via SPI, so the respective MOSI and MISO pins are connected with the Arduino’s MOSI and MISO pins along with 3.3 volts and ground.All of the wiring that connects to the Arduino is soldered to a protoboard that connects to all of the pins on the Arduino.

Firmware Design

Control signals are received from the transmitting radio on the glove by the receiver radio on the car, and then outputted through the serial bus to the Arduino R3 on the car. The Arduino R3 can then access the serial data and interpret it as a control signal, driving the motors appropriately. After decompiling the radio libraries in Arduino, and hand decoding the config options from the memory address given for its controller, we were able to customize it to our specifications:

  • By increasing the base clock speed, we were able to smooth out the latency from the glove control
  • By disabling auto-ack payloads and some other more obscure options, we stopped possible radio disconnects
  • By implementing automatic failure detection and reconnection states in our radio controllers, we were able to save ourselves a lot of hands-on debugging

Software Design

The software for this vehicle used the output signal from the flex sensors and converted that signal to an input signal for the motors. Depending on what the flex sensors measure, the wheels move forward, with various states of "bent" corresponding to several speeds for the motors, inluding a minimum under which the car comes to a stop. The glove also includes a gyroscope, which allows the user to steer the car left and right by turning their wrist in those respective directions.

Further Work

If we were to improve the car functionality, we would have added a reverse mode for the wheels. This could be accomplished using another flex sensor that, when it reads high, the wheels spin backward. There were many debugging features we could have added (LED’s, speakers) which would make testing much more streamlined. Additionally, we agree that the car would be improved if by adding some preprogrammed “dances” or maneuvers that could be activated by certain gestures, like vigorously shaking your hand.
